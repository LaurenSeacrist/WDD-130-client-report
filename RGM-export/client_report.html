CLIENT / DEVELOPER REPORT

STUDENT: Lauren Seacrist  ·  COURSE: WDD 130  ·  YEAR: 2026

⚠ GROUP SITUATION — IMPORTANT CONTEXT

This project required a group of students where each member would serve as Client once, Lead Developer once, and Junior Developer once across three different sites. The expectation was that the team would communicate regularly to assign roles, share repositories, and coordinate deliverables.

My group consisted of myself, Isaiah Sotutu, and Tad Hurley. From the beginning of the project, communication within the group was largely absent. Despite my attempts to reach out and establish a working plan, neither teammate consistently responded or proactively coordinated with me.

As a result, I made the decision to assign our roles myself in order to meet the course requirements. I served as the Client for NeuroPhoenix, the Lead Developer for the Retro Game Museum, and was assigned as Junior Developer on PixelForge (Tad's site, with Isaiah as lead). On the Retro Game Museum and PixelForge projects, I was forced to complete work independently because my teammates did not contribute. On PixelForge specifically, Isaiah — as lead developer — never communicated with me or granted me access to the repository, making it impossible for me to participate in my assigned junior role.

I want to be transparent about these circumstances. I put in the work to deliver complete, functional sites for all three projects and I believe the final products reflect that effort. The communication challenges described throughout this report are an honest account of what happened.

ROLE: CLIENT

CLIENT REPORT

// PROJECT: NEUROPHOENIX  ·  LEAD: ISAIAH SOTUTU  ·  JUNIOR: TAD HURLEY

How did you decide what content and images to use for your site plan?

For NeuroPhoenix I created a detailed site plan that outlined the purpose, target audience, color palette, and content structure I wanted the development team to follow. I selected imagery and copy that matched the brand identity I had in mind and documented everything clearly so Isaiah and Tad would have a strong reference to build from.

How did you communicate with your developer? Was there any issues with communication?

I provided my site plan and supporting materials to the development team, but communication throughout the project was inconsistent. Responses were infrequent and I had limited visibility into progress until near the end. While the team did ultimately complete the work, the lack of regular check-ins made the process more stressful than it needed to be. I would have appreciated more proactive updates from the lead developer.

Did your developer meet your expectations for your site?

The development team delivered a site that reflected the core requirements outlined in my site plan. There were refinements I would have liked to see, but given the communication challenges the team worked with what they had and produced a result that captured the overall direction I intended.

What will you do differently next time you hire a team to do a project for you?

Next time I would establish a shared communication channel from day one and set mandatory check-in milestones with clear deadlines. I would also make sure the team acknowledges the site plan early and has a chance to ask questions before development begins, rather than discovering misunderstandings at the end of the project.

ROLE: LEAD DEVELOPER

LEAD DEVELOPER REPORT

// PROJECT: RETRO GAME MUSEUM  ·  CLIENT: ISAIAH SOTUTU  ·  JUNIOR: TAD HURLEY

How did you manage your development team and communicate the tasks for your junior devs?

As lead developer on the Retro Game Museum I was responsible for building the home page and coordinating with Tad Hurley as the junior developer. I communicated the page assignments and shared the client's style guide and site plan materials. Despite this, Tad did not respond to communications and made no contributions to the project. Rather than submit an incomplete site to the client, I completed all pages — including the child pages that were Tad's responsibility — entirely on my own.

How did you communicate with your client? Was there any issues?

My communication with client Isaiah Sotutu was the most productive relationship in this project. He provided a detailed site plan, a style guide specifying exact colors and fonts (VT323 for headings, JetBrains Mono for body text, a navy/gold/blue/red palette), and content for each page. I shared progress updates and incorporated his feedback. The client was clear about his vision — a retro 80s/90s gaming aesthetic — and I worked to deliver exactly that across all four pages.

How did you handle publishing your client's site and sharing it with the client?

I exported the completed site as static HTML, CSS, and JavaScript files and deployed it using Netlify. The live site includes four fully styled and linked pages: Home, Legendary Consoles, About, and Contact. The live URL was shared directly with the client for review.

How was your experience with managing your site with GitHub?

I used GitHub for version control throughout the project. Working independently made branch management simpler, but it also highlighted how much easier a functioning team would have made the workload. I committed changes consistently and kept the repository organized as a professional habit, treating it as I would on a real development project.

ROLE: JUNIOR DEVELOPER

JUNIOR DEVELOPER REPORT

// PROJECT: PIXELFORGE  ·  CLIENT: TAD HURLEY  ·  LEAD: ISAIAH SOTUTU

How did you communicate with your team lead? Was there any issues?

On the PixelForge project I was assigned as a junior developer under lead developer Isaiah Sotutu. Isaiah did not communicate with me at any point during the project. I made multiple attempts to reach out to find out which pages I was responsible for and how to access the repository, but received no response. This was a direct consequence of the group communication breakdown described at the top of this report. Without direction or repository access from the lead, I had no way to participate in my assigned junior role.

Did you know what was expected of you for each site?

I understood from the course requirements that as a junior developer I would be responsible for building a child page. However, because Isaiah never communicated a task assignment or added me to the repository, I was unable to act on that understanding. I knew what my role should have been in theory, but in practice the lead developer's silence made it impossible to execute.

Were you able to contribute to your sites?

I was not able to contribute to PixelForge in my official junior developer capacity due to the lack of communication from the lead. To ensure the client (Tad Hurley) still had a complete deliverable, I independently built the entire PixelForge site — including both the home page and the about page — outside of my assigned role. I felt a responsibility to make sure the project was finished even though I was not given the support I needed to fulfill my junior role properly.

Did you run into any issues with GitHub and adding your changes to the sites?

Because Isaiah never added me to the PixelForge GitHub repository, I had no opportunity to use version control in my junior developer capacity on this project. This was a direct result of the lead developer failing to onboard me. In a professional environment, a lead developer is responsible for setting up repository access for all team members at the start of the project. That step was never taken here.

GENERAL REFLECTIONS

GENERAL EXPERIENCE

// COURSE: WDD 130  ·  2026

What was the most challenging part of this project? Were you able to overcome it?

Without question, the most challenging part was carrying the full workload of an entire team on my own. The group structure required that roles be shared and that each person contribute meaningfully. In reality, communication from my teammates was largely absent and I ended up doing the work of multiple roles simultaneously. I overcame this by staying organized, prioritizing deliverables, and committing to quality work regardless of the circumstances. It was not easy, but I refused to let the lack of teamwork result in an incomplete project.

What did you enjoy most about this project?

Despite the challenges, I genuinely enjoyed the creative process. Building the Retro Game Museum was especially rewarding — the 80s/90s arcade aesthetic gave me a lot to work with, and seeing a polished, multi-page site come together from a style guide and a set of content requirements was deeply satisfying. Knowing that I built something that looks and functions like a real product entirely on my own made the finished result feel earned.

What would you do differently if you could do the project again?

I would raise the communication issue with my instructor much earlier. Rather than absorbing all of the work myself and hoping things would improve, I should have flagged the problem in week one. I would also advocate for a required group agreement at the start — a document where all members commit to response times, deadlines, and how work will be distributed. Accountability needs to be built into the process from the beginning, not added after problems arise.

Do you feel more confident in working on real-world projects after this experience? Explain.

Yes — more confident than I expected. This project gave me hands-on experience with the complete lifecycle of web development: planning, designing, coding, version control, and deployment. I did it under real constraints with real deliverables. The team challenges, frustrating as they were, also taught me something valuable: in the real world, projects do not always have ideal conditions. Professionals are expected to deliver anyway. I did that here, and that gives me genuine confidence going forward.
